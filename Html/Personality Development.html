SECTION 01 / 07 · DEFINITION

What Is Artificial Intelligence?

AI is the science of creating machines that can perform tasks which normally require human intelligence — like learning, reasoning, problem-solving, understanding language, and recognising patterns.

🧠

Human-Like Thinking

AI mimics cognitive functions — learning from data, adapting to new inputs, and making decisions without being explicitly programmed for every case.

📈

Data-Driven

The more data AI systems process, the smarter they become. Modern AI is trained on billions of examples — text, images, speech, and sensor data.

⚡

Superhuman Speed

AI can analyse millions of data points in milliseconds — far beyond any human capability — enabling real-time decisions in medicine, finance, and security.

SECTION 03 / 07 · TAXONOMY

Types of Artificial Intelligence

AI exists on a spectrum — from narrow tools that do one thing brilliantly, to the theoretical general intelligence that could match (or exceed) humans in every domain.

🎯

NARROW AI (ANI)

Designed for one specific task. Examples: Spotify recommendations, spam filters, face unlock. This is what exists today.

Status: ✅ Widely deployed
🤖

GENERAL AI (AGI)

A machine with the ability to understand, learn, and apply intelligence across any task — just like a human. The holy grail of AI research.

Status: 🔬 Under research
🌐

SUPER AI (ASI)

A hypothetical AI that surpasses all human intelligence in every field — science, creativity, social skills. Could be humanity's greatest achievement or greatest risk.

Status: 🔮 Theoretical future

SECTION 06 / 07 · TIMELINE

AI Through Time

From a thought experiment in 1950 to systems that write code and generate art — the journey of AI is one of the fastest technological progressions in human history.

1950
Alan Turing's Test
Turing proposes the famous "Imitation Game" — the first formal framework for evaluating machine intelligence.
1956
AI is Born
John McCarthy coins the term "Artificial Intelligence" at the Dartmouth Conference — officially founding the field.
1997
Deep Blue Beats Kasparov
IBM's Deep Blue defeats world chess champion Garry Kasparov — a watershed moment for AI capability perception.
2011
Watson & Siri
IBM Watson wins Jeopardy! Apple launches Siri — AI enters the consumer mainstream for the first time.
2016
AlphaGo Moment
DeepMind's AlphaGo defeats world Go champion Lee Sedol — a game considered beyond AI just years earlier.
2020
GPT-3 & Transformers
OpenAI's GPT-3 (175B parameters) demonstrates near-human language generation, triggering the modern LLM revolution.
2022–23
ChatGPT Goes Viral
ChatGPT reaches 100 million users in 60 days — the fastest product adoption in history. AI enters every household.
2024–25
Agentic AI Era
AI agents that can autonomously plan, use tools, write code, and complete multi-step tasks without human intervention arrive commercially.
SECTION 07 / 07 · RESPONSIBILITY

The Ethics of AI

As AI grows more powerful, the ethical framework governing it becomes the most important question of our generation. Who is responsible when AI causes harm?

⚖️

FAIRNESS

AI systems trained on biased data reproduce and amplify those biases at scale — affecting hiring, lending, policing, and healthcare access.

👁️

TRANSPARENCY

Many AI decisions are "black boxes" — no one, not even their creators, can fully explain why they reached a specific conclusion. This is called the explainability problem.

🔒

PRIVACY

AI systems require massive amounts of personal data. The collection, storage, and use of this data raises serious questions about individual rights and consent.

🧭

ACCOUNTABILITY

When an autonomous AI vehicle causes a fatal accident, or an AI judge gives a biased sentence — who is legally and morally responsible?
